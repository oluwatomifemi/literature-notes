Things To Know Before Reading

  1. The handmaid’s tale is a dystopian novel written by Margaret Atwood and was first published in 1985.

  2. Margaret Atwood had this to say about the events of the handmaid’s tale; “As with The Handmaid’s Tale, I didn’t put in anything that we haven’t already done, we’re not already doing, we're seriously trying to do, coupled with trends that are already in progress... So all of those things are real, and therefore the amount of pure invention is close to nil.”

  3. The theme of women’s rights, power, and hypocrisy is explored throughout the novel. The women are given little to no say about what happens to them. In contrast, the males who have consolidated all the power in this society, on the basis on moral uprightness, live very hypocritical lives.

  4. All the handmaids in the novel are not referred to by their original name. The name used to address them is derived from a combination of two words: “Of” + their commander’s name. This naming system reduces them to the status of property.

Setting

Set in a hauntingly vivid and chillingly plausible future, "The Handmaid's Tale" immerses readers in the dystopian Republic of Gilead, a society built upon the ruins of the United States. This oppressive world is meticulously crafted with meticulous attention to detail, painting a picture of a totalitarian regime where every aspect of life is tightly controlled. The setting exudes an aura of palpable fear and surveillance, with the omnipresent Eyes constantly monitoring and punishing any signs of dissent. Gilead is a place where women are stripped of their autonomy, reduced to their reproductive capabilities, and assigned rigid roles based on their social class. The atmosphere is suffused with an eerie mix of religious fanaticism and moralistic oppression, where the distorted interpretations of scripture serve as a weapon of control. The architectural landscape reflects the regime's desire to erase the past, as old landmarks are repurposed or demolished, leaving behind a disorienting blend of the familiar and the unfamiliar. Against this backdrop of despair and societal decay, the characters navigate a treacherous world where trust is a luxury and rebellion comes at a devastating cost. The setting of "The Handmaid's Tale" serves as a potent metaphor for the fragility of freedom, the enduring power of hope, and the indomitable human spirit in the face of extreme adversity.

Summary

"The Handmaid's Tale" by Margaret Atwood is a compelling and thought-provoking dystopian novel that unveils a chillingly plausible future. Set in the totalitarian Republic of Gilead, a nation born out of the ashes of the United States, the story unfolds through the eyes of Offred, a handmaid assigned to bear children for the ruling elite. In this deeply oppressive society, women are stripped of their autonomy and reduced to their reproductive functions, their worth solely determined by their ability to conceive. Offred navigates a treacherous path, treading carefully between the demands of her oppressive masters, her longing for freedom, and her memories of a life that was once hers.

As the narrative progresses, Atwood peels back the layers of this dystopian world, revealing the haunting intricacies of Gilead's social structure and the oppressive mechanisms that keep its citizens in line. Offred's encounters with other handmaids, her secretive relationship with the Commander, and her forbidden connections with rebellious underground movements highlight the complexities and contradictions of this brutal regime.

Through Atwood's compelling prose, the reader is confronted with the stark realities faced by women in Gilead, as well as the wider implications of a society ruled by patriarchal domination and religious extremism. Themes of power, control, gender oppression, and the resilience of the human spirit intertwine, forcing readers to question the fragility of freedom and the potential consequences of complacency.

"The Handmaid's Tale" serves as a chilling cautionary tale, a stark reminder of the fragility of individual rights and the dangers of ideological extremism. Atwood's masterful storytelling, intricate world-building, and hauntingly realistic characters immerse readers in a future that feels disturbingly close to home. The novel stands as a powerful critique of oppressive systems, urging us to confront the potential consequences of apathy and ignorance. Through Offred's journey, we are reminded of the enduring strength of the human spirit and the indomitable will to resist, even in the face of unimaginable adversity.

Themes

The Handmaid's Tale explores several thought-provoking themes:

  • Gender Oppression: The novel delves into the oppressive treatment of women, highlighting the dehumanization, objectification, and loss of agency they experience under the patriarchal regime of Gilead.

  • Power and Control: The theme of power is central, as the ruling elite use manipulation, surveillance, and violence to maintain control over the population, particularly targeting women's bodies and reproductive capabilities.

  • Loss of Freedom: The novel examines the devastating consequences of a society that strips individuals of their basic rights and freedoms, emphasizing the importance of autonomy and self-determination.

  • Resistance and Rebellion: It explores the indomitable spirit of resistance, as characters navigate clandestine networks, engage in small acts of defiance, and strive to reclaim their agency, offering a glimmer of hope amidst the bleakness.

  • Social and Religious Extremism: Atwood's work confronts the dangers of religious fundamentalism taken to the extreme, illustrating the potential for oppressive ideologies to thrive and reshape society.

  • Memory and Identity: The theme of memory emerges as a powerful force, as Offred clings to her past, preserving her sense of self and humanity, while grappling with the erasure of her identity in Gilead.

  • Survival and Resilience: The novel explores the strength and resilience of the human spirit, as characters navigate a hostile world, adapting, strategizing, and finding ways to survive in the face of immense adversity.

  • Social Conformity: The pressure to conform and the consequences of deviating from societal norms are explored, highlighting the suffocating effect of strict adherence to prescribed roles and ideologies.

Through these profound themes, Margaret Atwood challenges readers to reflect on the dangers of complacency, the significance of individual agency, and the importance of actively resisting oppressive systems that threaten basic human rights and freedoms.

Characters

The Handmaid's Tale features a diverse cast of characters, each playing a distinct role in the narrative:

  • Offred (June): The protagonist and narrator, Offred is a Handmaid assigned to Commander Fred and his wife Serena. Her struggles, thoughts, and memories provide insight into the oppressive world of Gilead.

  • Commander Fred: The high-ranking official who plays a significant role in Offred's life. He embodies the power and privilege of the ruling class, while also harboring secrets and complex motivations.

  • Serena Joy: Commander Fred's wife, Serena Joy is a former televangelist and an influential figure in Gilead. Despite her participation in the establishment of the regime, she faces her own struggles and desires.

  • Aunt Lydia: A severe and authoritative figure, Aunt Lydia is responsible for training and overseeing the Handmaids. She embodies the indoctrination and enforcement of Gilead's oppressive regime.

  • Nick: The Commander's chauffeur and Offred's love interest, Nick operates within the system while harboring secrets and showing glimpses of compassion and rebellion.

  • Moira: Offred's best friend from the time before Gilead, Moira is a resilient and defiant character who becomes a symbol of resistance and hope.

  • Janine: Another Handmaid, Janine's story highlights the devastating effects of Gilead's oppressive system on women's mental and emotional well-being.

  • Commander Joseph Lawrence: A high-ranking official who provides a more complex and enigmatic portrayal of a member of the ruling class, challenging the simplistic categorizations of characters in Gilead.

  • Ofglen: Initially Offred's companion and fellow Handmaid, Ofglen reveals herself to be a member of an underground resistance movement, showcasing the complexity of allegiances and hidden networks.

These characters, with their diverse motivations, struggles, and interactions, contribute to the rich tapestry of Margaret Atwood's dystopian world, highlighting the various ways individuals navigate and resist oppression in Gilead.

Symbol

The Handmaid's Tale employs several powerful symbols throughout the narrative, adding depth and layers of meaning to the story:

  • The Handmaid's Red Robe and White Winged Bonnet: The distinctive clothing worn by the Handmaids symbolizes their reduced status and serves as a constant reminder of their assigned roles as reproductive vessels.

  • The Eyes: Represented by the recurring symbol of an eye, the Eyes symbolize the surveillance and control exerted by the oppressive regime of Gilead. They instill fear and paranoia among the characters, emphasizing the loss of privacy and individual freedom.

  • The Ceremony: This ritualistic act, where the Handmaids are forced to participate in a state-sanctioned sexual encounter with their assigned Commanders, symbolizes the objectification of women's bodies and the dehumanization inherent in the regime's control over reproduction.

  • The Scrabble Game: Offred's secret interactions with the Commander, where they play the forbidden game of Scrabble, represent a small pocket of rebellion, intellectual stimulation, and a connection to a world before Gilead's rise to power.

  • The Bible: The book, selectively interpreted and enforced by Gilead's leaders, serves as a symbol of manipulation and justification for their oppressive regime. It highlights the danger of using religious texts to enforce extremist ideologies.

  • The Red Center: The training facility for the Handmaids, the Red Center, represents a place of indoctrination, control, and subjugation. It symbolizes the systematic erasure of women's autonomy and the enforcement of rigid gender roles.

  • The Wall: The Wall, where the bodies of executed dissenters are displayed, serves as a chilling reminder of the consequences of resistance and dissent. It symbolizes the regime's brutality and the erasure of individual identity.

These symbols, woven into the fabric of The Handmaid's Tale, enhance the themes of oppression, control, rebellion, and the resilience of the human spirit. They provide readers with powerful visual and metaphorical representations of Gilead's dystopian world.

Allusions

The Handmaid's Tale incorporates various allusions to historical events, literature, and religious texts. These allusions add layers of meaning and cultural resonance to the narrative. References to historical events like the Salem witch trials and totalitarian regimes caution against religious fanaticism and the erosion of human rights. Literary allusions to works such as 1984 and Brave New World establish connections to dystopian societies, exploring themes of government control and the suppression of individuality. The novel also draws upon biblical stories, such as Rachel and Leah, and the practice of assigning handmaids to infertile wives, highlighting the distortion of religious scripture and the subjugation of women. These allusions invite readers to contemplate the profound interplay between history, literature, and religious symbolism within the story, deepening our understanding of its themes and societal critique.
