Things To Know Before Reading

  • Historical Context: Understanding the political and social backdrop of Shakespeare's time, particularly the Elizabethan era, can provide valuable insights into the play's themes and characters.

  • Revenge Tragedy: Hamlet falls into the genre of revenge tragedy, a popular form during Shakespeare's time. Familiarize yourself with the conventions and characteristics of this genre to appreciate the play's structure and narrative.

  • Characters: Get acquainted with the main characters of Hamlet, such as Prince Hamlet himself, King Claudius, Queen Gertrude, Ophelia, and the enigmatic figure of the ghost. Understanding their relationships, motivations, and complexities will enhance your reading experience.

  • Themes and Motifs: Hamlet explores various themes like revenge, madness, mortality, and the nature of power. Familiarize yourself with these overarching ideas and the recurring motifs throughout the play to grasp its deeper meanings.

  • Soliloquies: Hamlet's soliloquies offer profound insights into his thoughts and inner turmoil. Pay close attention to these introspective monologues as they provide a window into the character's psyche and dilemmas.

  • Language and Wordplay: Shakespeare's language can be challenging, but it is also rich with wordplay, metaphors, and poetic devices. Take the time to appreciate the beauty and intricacy of the language to fully immerse yourself in the play's brilliance.

  • Interpretations and Critical Analysis: Hamlet has been subject to countless interpretations and critical analyses over the years. Exploring different scholarly perspectives and interpretations can deepen your understanding and appreciation of the play.

Remember, approaching Hamlet with an open mind, curiosity, and a willingness to engage with its complexities will reward you with a profound and thought-provoking reading experience.

Setting

The play Hamlet is set in the kingdom of Denmark during the late medieval period. The setting primarily revolves around the royal court, the castle of Elsinore, where the political and personal drama unfolds. The atmosphere within the castle is one of intrigue, secrecy, and suspicion, creating a tense and claustrophobic environment. The castle's towering structure, with its imposing battlements and dark corridors, serves as a visual representation of the state of affairs in the play. The exterior settings include the haunting moors of Denmark and the eerie graveyard where pivotal moments take place. The contrast between the opulent courtly settings and the desolate landscapes further accentuates the themes of deception, corruption, and mortality explored throughout the play.

Summary

Hamlet is a tragic play written by William Shakespeare, believed to have been written between 1599 and 1601. The play follows the story of Prince Hamlet, who is mourning the death of his father, the King of Denmark. Hamlet's world is turned upside down when his mother, Queen Gertrude, hastily marries his uncle, Claudius, who becomes the new king. Haunted by the appearance of his father's ghost, who reveals that he was murdered by Claudius, Hamlet embarks on a quest for revenge.

Throughout the play, Hamlet grapples with his inner turmoil, contemplating the nature of life, death, and the morality of revenge. His actions and the consequences that unfold drive the plot forward, leading to a tragic and dramatic climax. Themes of deception, betrayal, madness, and the complexity of human nature are explored as Hamlet navigates a treacherous world of political intrigue and personal conflicts.

Hamlet is renowned for its rich language, memorable characters, and profound exploration of existential questions. It is one of Shakespeare's most celebrated works, often regarded as a masterpiece of world literature.

Themes

  • Appearance vs. Reality: Throughout the play, the theme of appearance versus reality is prominent. Characters often hide their true intentions behind a fa√ßade, leading to deception and confusion.

  • Revenge: The theme of revenge drives the actions of several characters in the play. Hamlet seeks revenge for his father's murder, while Laertes and Fortinbras also pursue vengeance for their own reasons.

  • Madness: Madness is a recurring theme in Hamlet, with the sanity of various characters questioned. Hamlet's feigned madness and Ophelia's actual descent into madness add layers of complexity to the play.

  • Mortality and the Fear of Death: The play explores human mortality and the fear of death. Hamlet contemplates the nature of life, death, and what lies beyond, contemplating the existential questions of existence.

  • Corruption and Decay: The theme of corruption and decay permeates the play, reflecting the decaying state of Denmark and the moral corruption of certain characters. This theme highlights the consequences of immoral actions.

  • Betrayal: Betrayal is a significant theme, with characters betraying one another's trust. Hamlet feels betrayed by his mother's hasty marriage, and his relationships with Rosencrantz and Guildenstern also involve betrayal.

  • Moral Dilemmas: Hamlet grapples with various moral dilemmas throughout the play, torn between his desire for revenge and his moral obligations. The play raises questions about the right course of action in difficult situations.

  • The Complexity of Human Nature: Hamlet delves into the complexity of human nature, portraying characters with both noble and flawed traits. The play explores the contradictions and intricacies of human behavior.

  • Fate vs. Free Will: The interplay between fate and free will is a recurring theme in Hamlet. Characters struggle with the idea of whether their actions are predetermined by fate or influenced by their own choices.

  • Identity and Self-Reflection: The play explores the theme of identity, as characters question their sense of self and grapple with issues of self-reflection. Hamlet, in particular, undergoes a journey of self-discovery.

  • The Power of Words and Language: Words and language hold significant power in the play. Characters use rhetoric and manipulation to convey messages, leading to misunderstandings and the manipulation of others.

Characters

  • Hamlet: The protagonist and Prince of Denmark. He is a complex character who struggles with grief, morality, and the desire for revenge.

  • King Claudius: Hamlet's uncle and the new king of Denmark. He is driven by ambition and power, having murdered his brother to seize the throne.

  • Queen Gertrude: Hamlet's mother and the queen of Denmark. She is torn between her loyalty to her son and her loyalty to her new husband, King Claudius.

  • Polonius: The chief counselor to King Claudius. He is a wise yet meddling character who frequently offers advice to others, including his children.

  • Ophelia: Polonius' daughter and Hamlet's love interest. She is a gentle and obedient young woman who becomes a victim of Hamlet's madness and her own circumstances.

  • Laertes: Polonius' son and Ophelia's brother. He is hot-tempered and seeks revenge for his father's death, becoming a foil to Hamlet.

  • Horatio: Hamlet's loyal friend and confidant. He is a rational and level-headed character who provides a sense of stability and support to Hamlet.

  • Ghost of King Hamlet: The ghost of Hamlet's father, who reveals that he was murdered by Claudius and urges Hamlet to seek revenge.

  • Rosencrantz and Guildenstern: Childhood friends of Hamlet who are summoned by Claudius to spy on Hamlet. They represent betrayal and manipulation.

  • Fortinbras: The Prince of Norway, whose father was slain by King Hamlet. He seeks to reclaim the lands lost by his father and serves as a contrast to Hamlet's indecisiveness.

Symbol

  • The Ghost: The appearance of the ghost of King Hamlet serves as a powerful symbol of the past and unresolved conflicts.

  • The Crown: Symbolizing power, the crown represents the authority and legitimacy of kingship.

  • The Poisoned Chalice: This symbolizes deceit and treachery.

  • The Play Within a Play: The play staged by Hamlet to expose Claudius' guilt symbolizes the power of theater and illusion.

  • The Skull: The skull of Yorick, the court jester, serves as a symbol of mortality and the inevitability of death.

  • The Garden: The concept of a garden symbolizes a place of innocence and purity.

  • The Mousetrap: The mousetrap play performed by Hamlet serves as a metaphor for catching the conscience of the king and exposing his guilt.

  • The Sword: Symbolizing violence and the tragic nature of the play, the sword represents the inevitable consequences of actions.

  • The Clothing: Clothing in the play serves as a symbol of deception and disguise, reflecting the theme of appearance versus reality.

  • The Darkness: Darkness symbolizes the state of moral corruption and the presence of evil in the world of the play.

Allusions

Allusions play a significant role in literature, and William Shakespeare's play Hamlet is no exception. Throughout the play, Shakespeare incorporates various allusions that enrich the narrative and add layers of meaning. One notable allusion in Hamlet is the frequent reference to Greek and Roman mythology. Characters often make allusions to mythological figures such as Hercules, Hecuba, and Niobe to convey their emotions, experiences, or to draw parallels to their own situations. These allusions not only provide cultural and historical context but also deepen the characterization and highlight the universal themes explored in the play.


Additionally, Shakespeare's use of biblical allusions adds another layer of meaning to the text. References to biblical stories and figures, such as Cain and Abel or the story of the Great Flood, serve as powerful metaphors and enhance the moral and religious undertones of the play. These allusions help to explore themes of sin, guilt, and redemption and provide a framework through which the audience can interpret the actions and dilemmas faced by the characters.


Furthermore, Hamlet contains allusions to classical literature and historical events. For instance, the character Polonius alludes to the ancient Greek philosopher Epictetus when he advises his son Laertes to "give thy thoughts no tongue." This reference to Epictetus emphasizes the theme of self-control and the importance of thinking before speaking. Shakespeare also alludes to historical events, such as the fall of Troy or the conquest of Julius Caesar, to draw parallels between past tragedies and the present conflicts in the play.


Overall, the extensive use of allusions in Hamlet adds depth, richness, and intertextuality to the text. These references to mythology, biblical stories, literature, and history provide a broader context for understanding the characters and their motivations, as well as the timeless themes explored in the play. The use of allusions not only engages the audience's knowledge and intellect but also enhances the overall artistic and literary value of Shakespeare's masterpiece.
