Things To Know Before Reading

Before delving into Arthur Miller's play "Death of a Salesman," there are several key aspects to keep in mind:

  1. American Dream: Understanding the concept of the American Dream and its significance in post-World War II America is crucial. The play explores the illusion of success and the pursuit of material wealth as an essential component of the American Dream.

  2. Tragedy: Familiarize yourself with the concept of tragedy in literature. "Death of a Salesman" is considered a modern American tragedy, highlighting the downfall of the protagonist and the underlying societal issues.

  3. Willy Loman: Get to know the central character, Willy Loman, a salesman who grapples with the pressures of his career, family expectations, and his own disillusionment. Understanding Willy's character and his internal conflicts is crucial to appreciating the play's themes.

  4. Father-Son Relationship: Explore the complexities of the father-son relationship depicted in the play, particularly between Willy and his sons, Biff and Happy. The strained relationship and its impact on their lives play a significant role in the narrative.

  5. Social and Economic Context: Gain insights into the social and economic climate of post-World War II America, where capitalism and the pursuit of success were highly valued. Understanding the influence of this context on the characters' motivations and actions enriches the reading experience.

  6. Symbolism: Be prepared to analyze symbolic elements employed in the play, such as Willy's car, the flute, or the seeds. These symbols convey deeper meanings and shed light on the characters' dreams, aspirations, and frustrations.

  7. Illusion vs. Reality: Explore the recurring theme of illusion versus reality in "Death of a Salesman." The play examines the blurred lines between dreams and harsh truths, and the characters' struggle to reconcile their aspirations with the reality of their lives.

  8. The American Family: Reflect on the portrayal of the American family in the play. Analyze the dynamics, expectations, and tensions within the Loman family, which reflect broader societal issues and the disillusionment that can arise within the nuclear family structure.

  9. Flashbacks and Nonlinear Narrative: Note the play's use of flashbacks and a non-linear narrative structure. Understanding the shifts in time and the significance of past events in shaping the present-day conflicts is essential to unraveling the story.

  10. Capitalism and Consumerism: Reflect on the critique of capitalism and consumerism present in the play. Consider the impact of the pursuit of material wealth on individuals and society as a whole, as well as the consequences of a society driven by commercial success.

By keeping these key points in mind, readers can approach "Death of a Salesman" with a deeper understanding of its themes, characters, and the social and cultural context that influenced its creation.

Setting

The play "Death of a Salesman" by Arthur Miller primarily takes place in two main settings:


1. The Loman Family Home: The majority of the play unfolds within the confines of the Loman family's home, located in Brooklyn, New York. The house serves as a physical representation of the Lomans' struggles, dreams, and disappointments. It is a modest, cramped space that reflects the financial instability and lack of success experienced by Willy Loman, the protagonist, in his career as a traveling salesman. The home also becomes a stage for the conflicts and tensions within the Loman family, as they navigate the challenges of their relationships and confront the disparity between their aspirations and reality.


2. Various Locations: Alongside the home setting, the play incorporates various other locations that reflect Willy's past, his imagination, and his travels. These settings are often depicted through flashbacks and memories. They include hotel rooms, restaurants, Willy's workplace, and the mindscapes of Willy's illusions. These additional settings contribute to the fragmented narrative structure of the play and provide insights into the characters' histories and the contrasting realities they face.


The contrasting environments of the Loman family home and the diverse locations visited throughout the play serve as backdrops for exploring the themes of the American Dream, identity, and the fragility of success in a capitalist society.

Summary

"Death of a Salesman" by Arthur Miller is a powerful and poignant play that delves into the life of Willy Loman, a traveling salesman in his declining years. The play revolves around Willy's pursuit of the elusive American Dream and his desperate struggle to find meaning and success in a changing society. As Willy grapples with his fading career, fractured family relationships, and personal disillusionment, the play explores themes of identity, the destructive nature of the capitalist system, and the impact of delusion and self-deception on human lives. Through a combination of realistic scenes, flashbacks, and symbolic elements, Miller skillfully exposes the flaws and contradictions of the American Dream, challenging the notion that material wealth equates to happiness and fulfillment. With its complex characters, poignant storytelling, and thought-provoking themes, "Death of a Salesman" remains a timeless examination of the human condition and the pursuit of happiness in a world of diminishing opportunities.

Themes

  • The American Dream and its elusive nature: "Death of a Salesman" explores the concept of the American Dream and the idea that success and happiness can be achieved through hard work and determination. However, the play also delves into the disillusionment and unattainability of this dream, questioning whether it is truly achievable for everyone.

  • The destructive impact of a materialistic society: The play examines the detrimental effects of a society driven by materialistic values. It highlights how the constant pursuit of wealth, status, and possessions can lead to emptiness, alienation, and the sacrifice of personal relationships and happiness.

  • The gap between aspirations and achievements: Willy Loman, the protagonist of the play, struggles with the disparity between his grand aspirations and his actual achievements. The play delves into the frustration, disappointment, and self-delusion that arise from this disconnect and the impact it has on an individual's self-esteem and sense of worth.

  • The questioning of the validity of the American Dream: "Death of a Salesman" raises profound questions about the validity and fairness of the American Dream. It challenges the notion that success and happiness are solely determined by financial prosperity and societal recognition, emphasizing the importance of personal fulfillment, authenticity, and meaningful connections.

  • The pursuit of success and its consequences: The play explores the relentless pursuit of success and the toll it takes on individuals and their relationships. It examines the sacrifices, compromises, and ethical dilemmas that arise when individuals prioritize achievement at all costs, shedding light on the emotional and psychological consequences of such a single-minded pursuit.

  • The exploration of happiness and self-worth in a capitalistic society: "Death of a Salesman" delves into the exploration of happiness and self-worth in a society driven by capitalist values. It questions whether true fulfillment can be found solely through financial success and challenges the notion that one's worth is solely determined by their professional achievements or societal standing.

Characters

  • Willy Loman: The protagonist of the play, a traveling salesman in his sixties who is struggling with disillusionment and regrets.

  • Linda Loman: Willy's loyal and supportive wife, who tries to maintain peace and stability within the family.

  • Biff Loman: Willy's eldest son, a former high school football star who is searching for his purpose in life and struggling with his relationship with his father.

  • Happy Loman: Willy's younger son, who works in a low-level job and is constantly seeking attention and validation.

  • Ben Loman: Willy's deceased older brother, who appears in Willy's memories and dreams as a successful and adventurous figure.

  • Charley: Willy's neighbor and friend, a successful businessman who serves as a contrast to Willy's struggles.

  • Bernard: Charley's son, who is studious and successful, highlighting the differences between him and Biff.

  • The Woman: A woman with whom Willy has an affair, representing Willy's betrayal of his marriage vows.

  • Howard Wagner: Willy's boss, who symbolizes the impersonal and ruthless nature of the business world.

  • Stanley: Happy's friend and colleague, who joins him in his pursuit of women and superficial success.

Symbol

  • The Loman House: Symbolizes the confinement and entrapment of Willy's dreams and aspirations.

  • The Seeds: Represent Willy's belief in the American Dream and the potential for success.

  • The Stockings: Symbolize Willy's infidelity and betrayal of his marriage.

  • The Car: Represents Willy's status and identity as a salesman, as well as his desire for freedom and escape.

  • The Diamonds: Symbolize material wealth and success, which Willy believes will bring happiness and fulfillment.

  • The Jungle: Represents the harsh and competitive nature of the business world.

  • The Flute: Symbolizes Willy's father and the idealized memories of his childhood.

  • The Tape Recorder: Represents Willy's desire to capture and relive moments from the past.

  • The Wire Recorder: Symbolizes the intrusion of technology and the loss of personal connection and authenticity.

  • The Rubber Hose: Represents Willy's inner turmoil and self-destructive tendencies.

Allusions

"Death of a Salesman" contains several allusions that add depth and meaning to the play. One notable allusion is the reference to the mythological figure of Narcissus. Willy Loman's excessive self-absorption and obsession with his own image mirror Narcissus, who fell in love with his own reflection. This allusion highlights Willy's delusions of grandeur and his inability to see the reality of his situation.


Another allusion in the play is the mention of Ben, Willy's successful and adventurous older brother. Ben represents the embodiment of the American Dream and serves as a constant reminder of what Willy aspires to be. The allusion to Ben emphasizes Willy's desire for financial success and the influence of societal ideals on his perception of self-worth.


Additionally, the character of Charley, Willy's neighbor, alludes to the biblical figure of Job. Like Job, Charley remains steadfast in the face of adversity and maintains his integrity despite Willy's struggles. This allusion highlights the theme of moral fortitude and contrasts Willy's flawed character with Charley's resilience.


Arthur Miller, the playwright, also alludes to the Great Depression and the challenges faced by the American working class during that era. The economic hardships of the time serve as a backdrop for Willy's struggles and contribute to the larger themes of disillusionment, social expectations, and the elusive nature of the American Dream.


These allusions in "Death of a Salesman" deepen the play's themes and characters, offering insights into the human condition, societal pressures, and the pursuit of success. They provide a richer context for understanding Willy Loman's tragic journey and the broader implications of his story.
