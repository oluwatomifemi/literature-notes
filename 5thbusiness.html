Setting

The setting of "Fifth Business" is primarily in small Canadian towns during the early to mid-20th century. The novel takes place in the fictional towns of Deptford, Dunstan's childhood home, and the neighboring towns of Toronto and Montreal. The rural landscapes and close-knit communities depicted in the novel reflect the cultural and social dynamics of small-town Canada during that time.

The contrasting settings of Deptford and the urban centers of Toronto and Montreal highlight the cultural shifts and social changes occurring in Canada during the period. Deptford represents a conservative and traditional community, deeply rooted in religious beliefs and superstitions. In contrast, Toronto and Montreal symbolize the growing urbanization and modernization of society, with their bustling streets, vibrant arts scene, and diverse population.

The World Wars and their aftermath also form an essential backdrop for the story, as the characters' lives are profoundly influenced by these historical events. The war-torn Europe and the soldiers returning with physical and emotional scars contribute to the themes of trauma, guilt, and the search for personal identity.

Overall, the setting of "Fifth Business" creates a rich and immersive backdrop that reflects the complexities of Canadian society, the impact of historical events, and the interplay between tradition and modernity.

Summary

"Fifth Business" is a thought-provoking novel written by Robertson Davies. The story revolves around the life of Dunstan Ramsay, a man reflecting on his experiences and relationships throughout his life. The narrative is framed as a letter from Ramsay to a headmaster, recounting key moments that shaped his identity.

The novel delves into themes of guilt, personal identity, and the blurred line between reality and illusion. Ramsay's life is intertwined with that of his childhood friend, Percy Boyd Staunton, and the enigmatic magician, Paul Dempster. Their complex relationships, shaped by a traumatic childhood incident, set the stage for a tale of redemption, fate, and the exploration of one's purpose in life.

Davies weaves together elements of Jungian psychology, mythology, and religion, making the novel a profound exploration of the human psyche. Through Ramsay's introspection, the readers are taken on a journey of self-discovery and the exploration of universal truths.

With its intricate storytelling, multidimensional characters, and profound themes, "Fifth Business" invites readers to contemplate the intricacies of human nature, the impact of past experiences on one's present, and the search for meaning in life.

  • Fate and Free Will: The concept of fate and free will is examined in "Fifth Business." The characters confront the idea that their lives may be shaped by predetermined events, but they also have the power to make choices that influence their destinies.

  • The Influence of the Past on the Present: The past has a profound impact on the present in "Fifth Business." The characters' histories and unresolved conflicts shape their lives and relationships, emphasizing the inescapable influence of the past.

  • Illusion and Reality: The theme of illusion and reality is explored as characters grapple with perceptions, truth, and the blurred boundaries between what is real and what is constructed or imagined.

  • The Search for Meaning and Purpose: Characters in the novel embark on a search for meaning and purpose in their lives, questioning their roles, beliefs, and the significance of their experiences.

  • Religion and Spirituality: The theme of religion and spirituality is present in "Fifth Business," as characters navigate their relationships with faith, religious institutions, and the spiritual dimensions of their lives.

  • Gender Roles and Expectations: The novel examines societal gender roles and expectations, exploring how these constructs influence the characters' lives, relationships, and self-perceptions.

  • Social Expectations and Conformity: The theme of social expectations and conformity is depicted as characters confront societal pressures and norms, challenging and conforming to these expectations in various ways.

Characters

  • Dunstan Ramsay: The protagonist and narrator of the story, Dunstan Ramsay is a complex character who serves as the "fifth business" in the lives of other characters. He is introspective and reflective, grappling with guilt, identity, and the search for meaning.

  • Paul Dempster (Boy Staunton): Paul is a pivotal character in the novel, initially known as Boy Staunton. He represents success and ambition but carries a dark secret that haunts him and others. His journey explores the themes of guilt, identity, and the consequences of one's actions.

  • Mary Dempster: Mary is a central character who undergoes a tragic event early in the story, leading to her becoming a figure of controversy and fascination. She represents innocence, resilience, and the power of forgiveness.

  • Liselotte Vitzliputzli (Liesl): Liesl is a mysterious and enigmatic woman who has a profound impact on Dunstan's life. She embodies elements of the mythological and represents the archetype of the "anima" in Carl Jung's psychology.

  • Padre Blazon: Padre Blazon is a Jesuit priest who plays a significant role in Dunstan's life. He represents religious authority and provides guidance and mentorship to Dunstan, particularly in matters of faith and spirituality.

  • Boy Staunton's Mother: Boy's mother is a domineering and influential figure in the story. Her presence and actions have lasting effects on both Boy and Dunstan, highlighting the complex dynamics of family and the power of maternal influence.

  • Amy Dempster: Amy is the wife of Dunstan's brother, and she carries her own secrets and tragedies. Her character explores themes of love, sacrifice, and the impact of past experiences on one's life.

  • Diana Marfleet: Diana is Dunstan's love interest and a nurse during World War I. Her character represents resilience, independence, and the ability to find purpose and strength amidst adversity.

  • Leola Cruikshank: Leola is a childhood friend of Dunstan's and later becomes Boy Staunton's wife. Her character highlights societal expectations and the consequences of conforming to prescribed gender roles.

  • Headmaster Colborne: Headmaster Colborne is Dunstan's mentor and a significant influence during his formative years. He embodies traditional values, discipline, and the pursuit of excellence.

Symbol

  • Snowball: Snowball is a recurring symbol in the novel, representing guilt and the burden of the past. It is associated with Dunstan's childhood incident with Mary Dempster and serves as a reminder of his unresolved guilt and the need for redemption.

  • Statues and Saints: Statues and saints appear throughout the story as symbols of religious beliefs, spiritual guidance, and the clash between traditional religious practices and personal experiences of faith.

  • The Stone in Mrs. Dempster's Pocket: The stone that Mary Dempster carries in her pocket is a symbol of her connection to the divine and her ability to heal others. It represents her spiritual power and the mysterious forces at work in the novel.

  • The Mummery: The mummery, a traditional folk play, is a symbol of disguise and the performance of various roles in life. It represents the masks people wear and the complexity of human nature.

  • Magician and Magic: The magician is a symbol of illusion, deception, and the power of transformation. It represents the duality of appearances and the hidden truths beneath the surface.

  • Stones and Pebbles: Stones and pebbles are recurring symbols that signify memory, truth, and the shaping of one's personal narrative. They represent the fragments of the past that shape the characters' identities.

  • Butterflies: Butterflies symbolize transformation, growth, and the pursuit of personal freedom. They reflect the characters' desire to break free from societal expectations and find their true selves.

  • World War I: World War I is a symbolic backdrop for the story, representing the destruction of innocence, the disillusionment of a generation, and the broader historical context that shapes the characters' lives.

  • Masks and Disguises: Masks and disguises serve as symbols of identity, concealment, and the performance of different roles in society. They explore the idea of personal authenticity and the facades people adopt in their interactions with others.

  • Fireworks: Fireworks symbolize moments of revelation, illumination, and personal awakening. They represent the explosive moments of self-discovery and the unveiling of hidden truths.

Allusions

Robertson Davies incorporates several allusions throughout "Fifth Business," enriching the narrative with references to various literary, historical, and mythological elements. These allusions add depth and resonance to the story, allowing readers to explore interconnected themes and ideas. Some notable allusions in the novel include:

  • The Myth of the Wandering Jew: The myth of the Wandering Jew, a figure cursed to roam the Earth until the second coming of Christ, serves as an allusion in the novel. It reflects the theme of eternal punishment, guilt, and the burden of past actions.

  • Mythological Figures: Davies references various mythological figures, such as Diana, Apollo, and Hermes, to explore themes of identity, fate, and the influence of ancient myths on human behavior.

  • Shakespearean Plays: Allusions to Shakespearean plays, including "Macbeth" and "King Lear," provide insights into the characters' motivations, internal conflicts, and moral dilemmas.

  • Carl Jung's Archetypes: The novel draws on Carl Jung's concept of archetypes, such as the Trickster and the Wise Old Man, to deepen the psychological exploration of the characters' personalities and motivations.

  • Myth of Persephone: The myth of Persephone, who descends into the underworld and returns transformed, resonates with the themes of personal growth, redemption, and the transformative power of life's trials.

  • Historical Events: Historical events, such as World War I and the sinking of the Lusitania, serve as allusions, providing a broader historical context and influencing the characters' experiences and perceptions of the world.

These allusions enhance the layers of meaning in "Fifth Business," inviting readers to explore the interplay between literature, history, mythology, and the complexities of human existence.
